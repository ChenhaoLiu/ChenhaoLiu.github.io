A Multi-Stage Approach to Contour Extraction


With Yuliang Guo, supervised by Prof. Benjamin Kimia


Introduction to this Project

Contour extraction is a key topic in computer vision, especially in topics related to object recognition and multi-view applications. In order to
extract curve fragments, which contains chains of ordered edge points, from images, we propose a multi-stage approach to curve extraction where the
curve fragment search space is iteratively reduced by removing unlikely candidates using geometric constrains, but without affecting recall, to a
point where the application of an objective functional becomes appropriate. The process progresses in stages from local to global: (i) edges, (ii) curvelets,
(iii) unambiguous curve fragments, (iv) resolving ambiguities to generate a full set of curve fragment candidates, (v) merging curve fragments based
on a learned photometric and geometric cues as well a novel lateral edge sparsity cue, and (vi) the application of a learned objective functional to
get a final selection of curve fragments. The process of our approach is shown in the figure below.

                        

Resolving Ambiguities in Contour Extraction

In the process of contour extraction, we try to resolve ambiguity of contours by group remaining edges. The first step is to construct a graph of all
possible groupings, which is called a Hypothesis Graph. We define an energy function of contours and divide the Hypothesis Graph into hundreds
of connected components for efficiency. A typical area of Hypothesis Graph is shown in the figure below, where the dashed lines are hypotheses.

                        

In each connect component, Dijkstra's algorithm is used to build a shortest path tree between each pair of nodes to link under the energy function.
Then the paths are ranked by their energy, then we add each path by its rank while avoiding overlap. After all connected components are solved,
our system move on to detect and link junctions to form a complete contour fragment graph, using a priority queue. The input and output of
